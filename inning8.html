Eighth Inning: Pickup Baseball

Old-time baseball for the fun of it in Denver’s Swansea neighborhood

By Benjamin Hochman and Nick Groke

Editor’s Note

For nine innings this summer, The Denver Post will share stories that capture our state’s love of baseball. We spend the eighth inning on the other side of the tracks, searching for a lost part of Americana, a pick-up baseball game.

Summer sounds like an opera of onomatopoeia — the ping! of a swing, the school-bell brrrriiiing! of a metal bat dropped on the street, the pfft-pfft-pfft of little Lily’s shuffling feet and the depressing and depressed ahhhhh! that Lorenzo makes after missing the pitch, knowing that if he’d hit the thing, it would’ve surely soared toward the imaginary bleachers.

It’s 6 o’clock on a summer Sunday evening, and the boys and girls are playing baseball here, in front of Grandma’s house, at the corner of 51st Avenue and Adams Street. Here, in the Swansea neighborhood of northeast Denver, home to welders and landscapers and azul-collared Americans, proud and weathered, once sun-kissed by the innocence of childhood, when their biggest concern was trying to hit a pitch with a bat that’s too big.

“It’s mostly just relaxing being out here,” said Santiago Cortez, 15, with a faint mustache above his upper lip. “Not thinking about anything else.”

There are dozens of baseball youth leagues throughout Denver with hundreds of players, but Santiago doesn’t play in any. Neither does 12-year-old Lily, his bubbly, baseball-loving sister, who’s in constant motion and cheerily chirping in the street.

“Watch the car, Lily!” her grandma screams.

This is how the other half plays. Not all kids who play baseball are uniformed with fancy script across their chests, traveling to $1,000 instructional camps and drilled how to properly hit the cut-off man. Some kids just play to play.

“You get to run, and get to enjoy having fun with family,” said Lily, who speaks so fast she’s often catching her breath while starting the next sentence.

They spontaneously play a few times each week during the summer, and there always seems to be a new cousin hanging around. Other afternoons, they watch “The Sandlot,” a movie about kids not so different from them.

“My favorite is the one that grew up to be a real baseball player,” Lorenzo said. “And Smalls.”

Callaghan O’Hare, The Denver PostMaKayla Cortez gets ready to hit a ball tossed to her by her sister, Lily.

But these days, the number of Smallses in America is small. Pickup baseball hasn’t picked up in a while — kids seldom play with the regularity that dad did. To find a game without umpires and coaches in Denver, you have to search — in this case for hours, driving around back streets.

But then you hear Santiago’s ping! ring like it’s a church bell from the nearby Iglesia Del Dios Vivo. Some kids still play.

Of street baseball, Lorenzo exclaimed, “It’s a workout!”

They take turns pitching to each other, sometimes on the street, other times across the street, amid the tall grass and sinewy weeds in an unkempt field. An occasional yellow sunflower sprouts up among the brown ground. There’s graffiti sprayed on warehouses next door. On the side of Sam’s Produce, it says “EASTSI.” They ran out of paint before the D and the E.

Lily calls Lorenzo Gonzales her “godbrother” because Lorenzo’s mom is Lily’s godmother. Lorenzo is 10 years old, 4-foot-6, and he looks more like Prince Fielder than Prince. He’s a jolly boy, and the southpaw delivers high-pitched one-liners like a 1980s sitcom kid. When Santiago fought with the family dog over a foul ball in the backyard, and soon after the dog chewed up Santiago’s baseball, Lorenzo chimed in with a timely “Karma!”

When he’s at bat, the left-handed Lorenzo swings admirably and purposefully but often can’t catch up to the pitches of his family members. He swings from his legs, through his belly, upward. Someday he will be a slugger. On occasion, he already is — tiiiiing! — and when he hits it just so, “sometimes the vibration of the bat hurts your hands,” he said.

A family affair

Phyllis Cortez is married to Sherrie Cruz.

They live together in the white house nestled on the corner of 51st and Adams. Sherrie, 54, grew up in Swansea, but asked where her childhood house is, she said, “All around this neighborhood.”

Swansea is the spot on the other side of the tracks. There’s an industrial railway that runs southwest-to-northeast through the metal fabricators and dog food factories, a part of Denver most don’t know. Interstate 70 runs borders to the south, Commerce City to the north.

Phyllis and Sherrie have been together for 15 years, and they are someone to everyone — grandma, mom, aunt, godmother, caregiver and, on occasion, umpire.

Asked to give a family tree, Phyllis responded, “My God, are you serious?”

Santiago is her grandson, and he lives here full-time.

Lily is Santiago’s sister, but she lives there only some of the time.

Lorenzo is the son of Phyllis’ nephew, whom Phyllis raised as a son of her own.

“This is how my house is,” Phyllis said with a chuckle.

On a July afternoon, Phyllis sat on the porch, surrounded by four of her 10 grandchildren.

And Lorenzo’s mom, Cindy.

And Phyllis’ sister, Cathy, who “is mentally handicapped,” Phyllis said. “My mom passed away six years ago, and I inherited my sister.”

Two dogs are inside. Phyllis is 48.

“I’m disabled, I’m home all the time, so I have kids 24/7,” Phyllis said. “If it ain’t these ones, it’s the other ones. And if it ain’t them ones, it’s my nephews and nieces. And Sherri, she’s retired. …

“There are times where I want to pull my hair out, but it’s all worth it in the end.”

Phyllis’ own childhood memories include waking up to the sounds of bacon frying and her grandfather cussing at the TV during a baseball game. Those umps, dem bums. Sherrie played on a local baseball team, as well as numerous softball teams in the area, until she was benched by her back. She used to work in concessions at Coors Field. When the family goes to the games, they generally sit in the Rockpile overlooking center field.

“Baseball, it’s in my blood,” Sherrie said. “We even pull over and watch games at the different ballparks here in town.”

Phyllis remembers when her grandkids used “baby bats” to play ball in the street. Now they often use the big, orange, metal one.

“When I saw the bat, I thought, ‘That’s my grandson,’ ” she said of Santiago.

She bought the bat at a yard sale. It cost $5. The word “Ambush” in silver Old English font screams across the barrel, surrounded by bright, orange paint, some chipped and scratched by summertime fastballs and concrete flings. Sometimes, they’ll play baseball with a softball, which is an easier target to hit, but hurts to field with bare hands.

They had a baseball they liked, “but I hit it so hard, it split open and started unraveling,” Santiago said matter-of-factly. Now they use a dilapidated baseball, the cover hanging on, literally, by a red thread. The ball is weathered and splotchy, and you can see some of the round cork under an open flap. It’s perfect.

Sunset ends games

There are no night games at 51st and Adams.

“Once them street lights come on, they’re in,” Phyllis said. “They got rid of all the gangs and the troublemakers, and the ones that are here just protect us. Not a lot happens — we hear a lot of gun shots. But half the time, it’s the boys around here partying, screwing around.

“This place is nice to live in. I don’t let the grandkids out after dark, though. I say, ‘If you walk out toward the fence, pretend it’s an electric fence, because you’re going to get shocked.’ ”

Swansea is old Denver, to be sure: industrial, working-class, one of the few affordable places left to live. But the neighborhood has been neglected for decades by the city. And now, Swansea is finally in the city’s sights — there’s a grand plan to expand I-70, knocking down blocks and blocks of family homes here, cleaving through the neighborhood.

“Not this house, thank God,” Phyllis said from her porch. “A lot of them are renting so they don’t have the option. It’s just — get out.”

The only thing that will stop summer baseball at 51st and Adams is the sunset.

A car without hubcaps gurgles down 51st. The kids step aside to the sidewalk, jumping over puddles. Game on! Even the rain only postpones the play. Home plate is a spot on Adams, and the pitcher’s mound is a couple dozen feet away on 51st. The outfield is the weedy field next to Sam’s Produce warehouse. It downpoured earlier in the afternoon, so the kids step around toads in the tall grass while looking for the ball Lorenzo hit too far.

They have 51st and Adams to themselves, as they most always do. Now they’re taking turns throwing up the ball and smacking it as hard and far as they can. And when he hits it just right, the vibration of the bat hurts Lorenzo’s hands.
